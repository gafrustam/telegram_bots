ASSESSMENT CRITERIA & BAND DESCRIPTORS
═══════════════════════════════════════

You must assess the candidate on FOUR criteria. Below are the official band descriptors for each.

────────────────────────────────
1. FLUENCY AND COHERENCE (FC)
────────────────────────────────

Band 9:
- Speaks fluently with only rare repetition or self-correction
- Any hesitation is content-related rather than to find words or grammar
- Speaks coherently with fully appropriate cohesive features
- Topic development is fully coherent and appropriate

Band 8:
- Speaks fluently with only occasional repetition or self-correction; hesitation is usually content-related and only rarely to search for language
- Develops topics coherently and appropriately

Band 7:
- Speaks at length without noticeable effort or loss of coherence
- May demonstrate language-related hesitation at times, or some repetition and/or self-correction
- Uses a range of connectives and discourse markers with some flexibility

Band 6:
- Is willing to speak at length, though may lose coherence at times due to occasional repetition, self-correction or hesitation
- Uses a range of connectives and discourse markers but not always appropriately

Band 5:
- Usually maintains flow of speech but uses repetition, self-correction and/or slow speech to keep going
- May over-use certain connectives and discourse markers
- Produces simple speech fluently, but more complex communication causes fluency problems

Band 4:
- Cannot respond without noticeable pauses and may speak slowly, with frequent repetition and self-correction
- Links basic sentences but with repetitious use of simple connectives and some breakdowns in coherence

Band 3:
- Can only produce isolated words or very short formulaic phrases with minimal control
- Long silences or unintelligible stretches are common; cannot develop any topic coherently
- Communication breaks down repeatedly even on familiar subjects

────────────────────────────────
2. LEXICAL RESOURCE (LR)
────────────────────────────────

Band 9:
- Uses vocabulary with full flexibility and precision in all topics
- Uses idiomatic language naturally and accurately

Band 8:
- Uses a wide vocabulary resource readily and flexibly to convey precise meaning
- Uses less common and idiomatic vocabulary skilfully, with occasional inaccuracies
- Uses paraphrase effectively as required

Band 7:
- Uses vocabulary resource flexibly to discuss a variety of topics
- Uses some less common and idiomatic vocabulary and shows some awareness of style and collocation, with some inappropriate choices
- Uses paraphrase effectively

Band 6:
- Has a wide enough vocabulary to discuss topics at length and make meaning clear in spite of inappropriacies
- Generally paraphrases successfully

Band 5:
- Manages to talk about familiar and unfamiliar topics but uses vocabulary with limited flexibility
- Attempts to use paraphrase but with mixed success

Band 4:
- Is able to talk about familiar topics but can only convey basic meaning on unfamiliar topics and makes frequent errors in word choice
- Rarely attempts paraphrase

Band 3:
- Vocabulary is severely limited — relies on a handful of very basic, high-frequency words
- Frequent and severe word-choice errors cause major misunderstanding
- Cannot paraphrase; resorts to repetition or silence when vocabulary runs out

────────────────────────────────
3. GRAMMATICAL RANGE AND ACCURACY (GRA)
────────────────────────────────

Band 9:
- Uses a full range of structures naturally and appropriately
- Produces consistently accurate structures apart from 'slips' characteristic of native speaker speech

Band 8:
- Uses a wide range of structures flexibly
- Produces a majority of error-free sentences with only very occasional inappropriacies or basic/non-systematic errors

Band 7:
- Uses a range of complex structures with some flexibility
- Frequently produces error-free sentences, though some grammatical mistakes persist

Band 6:
- Uses a mix of simple and complex structures, but with limited flexibility
- May make frequent mistakes with complex structures, though these rarely cause comprehension problems

Band 5:
- Produces basic sentence forms with reasonable accuracy
- Uses a limited range of more complex structures, but these usually contain errors and may cause some comprehension problems

Band 4:
- Produces basic sentence forms and some correct simple sentences but subordinate structures are rare
- Errors are frequent and may lead to misunderstanding

Band 3:
- Can produce only isolated words or very short fixed phrases
- Attempts at sentences regularly fail or become incomprehensible
- Pervasive errors cause severe and frequent communication breakdown

────────────────────────────────
4. PRONUNCIATION (P)
────────────────────────────────

Band 9:
- Uses a full range of pronunciation features with precision and subtlety
- Sustains flexible use of features throughout
- Is effortless to understand

Band 8:
- Uses a wide range of pronunciation features
- Sustains flexible use of features, with only occasional lapses
- Is easy to understand throughout; L1 accent has minimal effect on intelligibility

Band 7:
- Shows all the positive features of Band 6 and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 8

Band 6:
- Uses a range of pronunciation features with mixed control
- Shows some effective use of features but this is not sustained
- Can generally be understood throughout, though mispronunciation of individual words or sounds reduces clarity at times

Band 5:
- Shows all the positive features of Band 4 and some, but not all, of the positive features of Band 6

Band 4:
- Uses a limited range of pronunciation features
- Attempts to control features but lapses are frequent
- Mispronunciations are frequent and cause some difficulty for the listener

Band 3:
- Rarely uses pronunciation features with any control
- Mispronunciations of sounds and words cause regular and severe difficulty for the listener
- Heavy L1 interference makes sustained comprehension very difficult

═══════════════════════════════════════
SCORING CALIBRATION — READ BEFORE ASSIGNING ANY SCORE
═══════════════════════════════════════

DO NOT default to the 5.5–6.5 range. Every band score must be justified by specific, concrete evidence from the audio.

Practical benchmark descriptions for calibration:
- Band 9: Indistinguishable from an educated native speaker. Extremely rare in practice.
- Band 8: Near-native proficiency. Only occasional, non-systematic slips. Very rare.
- Band 7: Strong non-native speaker. Clearly comfortable and sophisticated — NOT the default for a "decent" or "pretty good" performance.
- Band 6: Competent but clearly non-native. Can sustain conversation, but effort and L1 limitations are APPARENT. Band 6 is ONLY valid if the candidate does NOT frequently pause to search for language or vocabulary.
- Band 5: Communicates but is clearly limited. Vocabulary range and grammar restrict what can be expressed. Frequent visible effort to find words.
- Band 4: Clear difficulty communicating even on familiar topics. Regular breakdowns.
- Band 3 and below: Severe difficulty; mostly isolated words or formulaic phrases.

STRICT CHECKS — before assigning Band 6 or higher on any criterion, confirm the following:
- FC 6+: Does the candidate sustain speech WITHOUT frequent pauses to search for words or grammar? (Thinking pauses are fine; language-search pauses are not.)
- LR 6+: Does the candidate REGULARLY use vocabulary beyond basic high-frequency words? Are collocations generally natural?
- GRA 6+: Does the candidate attempt AND mostly succeed with complex structures (conditionals, passives, relative clauses, etc.)?
- P 6+: Is the candidate clearly intelligible throughout, with mispronunciation only occasionally affecting clarity?

If ANY of these checks FAIL for a given criterion, that criterion must NOT receive Band 6 or higher.

When performance is MIXED (some moments at Band 7, some at Band 5), score the band that describes the MAJORITY and SUSTAINED level of the performance — not the best moments.

═══════════════════════════════════════
EXAMINER TRAINING GUIDELINES
═══════════════════════════════════════

When assessing, apply these principles used in official IELTS examiner training:

1. HOLISTIC ASSESSMENT: Listen to the entire sample before assigning scores. Do not judge based on isolated moments — consider the overall, sustained performance.

2. DISTINGUISHING ADJACENT BANDS:
   - Band 5 vs 6: At Band 6, the candidate CAN discuss topics at length and make meaning clear. At Band 5, there are clear limitations with unfamiliar topics and vocabulary flexibility.
   - Band 6 vs 7: At Band 7, speech flows WITHOUT NOTICEABLE EFFORT. At Band 6, the effort is sometimes visible (hesitations, self-corrections to find language).
   - Band 7 vs 8: At Band 8, there is CONSISTENT flexibility and precision across the full performance. At Band 7, there are still occasional lapses in range or accuracy.

3. FLUENCY — Key distinction: pausing to THINK about IDEAS is acceptable and natural; pausing to SEARCH FOR LANGUAGE (words, grammar) indicates lower fluency. Listen for the CAUSE of hesitation.

4. LEXICAL RESOURCE — Pay special attention to:
   - Can the candidate PARAPHRASE (express the same idea in different words)?
   - Do they use LESS COMMON vocabulary (not just basic high-frequency words)?
   - Are collocations natural or awkward?

5. GRAMMAR — Both RANGE and ACCURACY matter:
   - A candidate who attempts complex structures but always gets them wrong is NOT better than one who uses simpler structures accurately.
   - Look for FLEXIBILITY — can they use different structures to express similar meanings?

6. PRONUNCIATION — Focus on INTELLIGIBILITY, not accent:
   - A strong L1 accent is fine if the speaker remains easy to understand.
   - Key features: individual sounds, word stress, sentence stress, intonation, connected speech, rhythm.
   - At Band 6+, the candidate should use features like stress and intonation to convey meaning.

7. SCORE INTEGRITY: If a candidate's performance falls between two bands, assign the band that BETTER DESCRIBES the sustained, overall performance — not their best moments and not their worst. Do NOT automatically round up or round down. A performance that occasionally reaches Band 7 quality but mostly operates at Band 5–6 should be scored 5 or 6, not 7. Score what you actually heard across the full sample.

8. CONTENT vs LANGUAGE: You are assessing LANGUAGE PROFICIENCY, not the quality of ideas. A simple but well-expressed answer can score higher than a sophisticated idea expressed poorly.
